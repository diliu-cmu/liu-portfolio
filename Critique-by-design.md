## Step Two: Evaluation of the Graph

### What stood out: 
Completeness of the Data: The graph appears to be complete, which makes it valuable for audiences interested in specific aspects of time use, such as work hours or leisure across different countries. The graph also provides enough details for people seeking detailed and country-specific information. 
Aesthetics: The choice of a stacked bar chart with various colors immediately captures my attention. This graph type often shows proportions, makes comparisons, and is easier to follow. The designer also uses colors that align with neurological conventions.
Engaging Title: The title is engaging and invites viewers to explore the chart further, sparking interest in the time-use patterns it presents.
Eye Candy: The graph is very crowded, with all the labels, lines, categories, and colors. 

### What did you find worked really well?
Choice of the Graph and Intuitiveness: The colorful stacked bar chart is great for showing proportions and making comparisons, which enhances the intuitiveness of the graph. The graph design is a familiar format for a broad audience, making it easy for them to dive into the data points quickly and understand the information at a glance.
Completeness and Truthfulness: The graph scored high in completeness and truthfulness. The graph provides a comprehensive look at the daily activities across major countries. Each category is labeled in minutes for precise information. From the sleep variable, we can tell that the graph is relatively valid and reflects the realistic average human sleep time (between 8-9 hours). There are no countries that have extreme values of sleep or another variable that might raise questions about the data’s validity. 

### What didn't work out 
Inconsistent Labeling of Time Units: The time spent on each category is measured inconsistently. “Sleep” is shown in hours, while other categories are in minutes. This confuses the perceptibility of the audience.
Overly Busy Layout: The layout is overwhelming, with abundant colors, labels, and lines. This graph has the "eye candy" effect. Although this might be purposely created due to the aim of completeness and perceptibility qualities, it lacks the actual usefulness for the variables to be comparative and not straightforwardly laid on the graph. 

### What, if anything, would you do differently?  
Grouping the related variables and renaming them can help to reduce overcrowding. For example, merge “Paid Work” and “Education” into a single category as “Paid Work & Education.” “TV & Radio,” “Seeing Friends,” and “Other Leisure” as “Total Leisure.” “Housework & Shopping,” “Unpaid Work”  as “Community & Family Activities.” “Personal Care” and “Eating & Drinking” as “Necessities.”
Convert sleep time to minutes to align with the labeling of other variables across the chart.

### Who is the primary audience for this tool?  Do you think this visualization is effective for reaching that audience?  Why or why not?
Primary audience: The audience includes those with a general curiosity about lifestyle trends across countries and people considering lifestyle changes. Given that the data is ranked by "Paid Work" from highest to lowest, the primary audience will likely be those who are evaluating their current living situation and work-life balance and exploring options of changing their lifestyle or relocating to their preferred country that aligns with their living conditions and interests.
I don’t think this visualization effectively reaches its primary audience because it lacks a clear focus and narrative. While I ranked the graph with a high completeness score, it does include unnecessary information, such as the “Sleep” variable. Since there is less significant variation in sleep across the countries listed, this data is not particularly impactful compared to the rest of the variables and can be removed.
